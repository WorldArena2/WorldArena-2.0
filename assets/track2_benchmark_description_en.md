# WorldArena 2.0 Track 2: World Model Data Engine Track

> Document version: 1.0  
> For the service integration protocol, see the [Candidate World Model Service API](track2_world_model_service_api_en.md).

## 1. Track Objective

Track 2 evaluates the practical value of an action-conditioned world model (WM) as a data engine for robot model-based reinforcement learning (MBRL).

Participants submit neither a robot policy nor a reward model. Instead, they submit a world-model inference service that can be called by the official evaluator. Given a visual history, optional state and language conditions, and a sequence of future actions, the service shall generate a future visual sequence aligned step by step with those actions. The organizer uses the generated results for policy rollout, reward estimation, and policy optimization, and then evaluates the optimized policy in an independent held-out environment.

The central question of this track is:

> Under a fixed training budget, can interaction data generated by the world model reliably improve a robot policy, and does that improvement transfer to an independent evaluation environment?

Visual appeal is not the final objective. Temporal consistency, action controllability, and task-relevant dynamics should ultimately translate into effective support for policy learning.

## 2. Evaluation Targets and Responsibilities

### 2.1 Participants

- Host or package a world-model service that conforms to the public API;
- Generate future RGB frames conditioned on the observation history and actions in each request;
- Respond reliably within the specified resource, latency, and availability budgets;
- Ensure reproducible behavior for the same model version and random seed;
- Declare the service capabilities, model version, and required third-party dependencies.

### 2.2 Official Evaluator

- Provide initial conditions, candidate actions, and any required task conditions;
- Apply a uniform action adapter, policy rollout procedure, reward computation, and MBRL update;
- Fix the policy starting point, optimization algorithm, and interaction budget across submissions;
- Evaluate the updated policy in a held-out environment isolated from participant services;
- Record protocol compliance, valid-sample rate, runtime performance, and evaluation results.

### 2.3 Interface Boundary

The participant service is responsible only for predicting future visual observations. It shall not return or replace actions, rewards, success decisions, termination signals, or final scores produced by official components. It does not need access to official policy parameters, the reward model, the held-out evaluation environment, or non-public data. Custom diagnostic fields in a response are not used for policy training or ranking.

## 3. Evaluation Procedure

```text
Official initial conditions
  -> Robot policy with a fixed starting point generates candidate actions
  -> Participant world-model service generates future video
  -> Official reward and MBRL pipeline updates the policy
  -> Updated policy is evaluated in an independent held-out environment
  -> Policy utility, robustness, and service validity are aggregated
```

Evaluation uses closed-loop, multi-round rollouts of action chunks. A later request may use output generated in the preceding round as its new visual history. The model must therefore control drift over long-horizon rollouts, rather than merely generate a single video segment.

To ensure fairness, the organizer fixes the following for all submissions:

- Initial policy checkpoint;
- Hidden reset samples and their grouping;
- Policy, sampling, and training random seeds;
- MBRL algorithm, reward module, optimizer, and training budget;
- Number of world-model requests, maximum prediction horizon, and runtime resource budget;
- Final evaluation environment, initial conditions, and evaluation seeds.

During training, actions are generated dynamically by the current policy; a complete trajectory is not fixed in advance. Different world models may produce different future observations, and an updated policy may consequently produce different subsequent actions. These closed-loop differences are precisely what this track is intended to evaluate.

## 4. Inputs and Outputs

The canonical input for each prediction sample includes:

- RGB history frames ordered chronologically from oldest to newest;
- Action history corresponding to transitions between history frames;
- The future action sequence to be predicted;
- An optional robot-state history;
- An optional natural-language task description;
- An organizer-specified random seed and opaque request identifiers.

The canonical output includes:

- Predicted RGB frames aligned step by step with the future actions;
- Service-version and request-correlation information;
- Optional runtime diagnostics that are not used for scoring.

The encoding, shape, alignment, and null-value rules for images, actions, and states are defined by the [API document](track2_world_model_service_api_en.md). The organizer will provide the profiles that must be supported before evaluation. Public examples are for interface integration only and do not represent the held-out evaluation distribution.

## 5. Evaluation Criteria

### 5.1 Downstream Policy Utility

The primary evaluation criterion is the performance of a policy in the held-out environment after MBRL using the participant's world model, together with the improvement over the policy before optimization. The final evaluation is based primarily on closed-loop policy return; neither standalone frame-reconstruction metrics nor perceived video quality substitute for that objective.

### 5.2 Action Conditioning and Long-Horizon Robustness

The organizer will verify that different action sequences produce plausible, distinguishable, and temporally continuous results, and will assess error accumulation, state degradation, and sensitivity to anomalies during multi-round closed-loop generation.

### 5.3 Generalization and Consistency

Evaluation covers held-out initial conditions, task conditions, and random seeds. A model shall not infer prediction content from sample order, request identifiers, or fixed public examples.

### 5.4 Service Validity

Protocol pass rate, valid-response rate, latency, timeouts, reproducibility, and resource compliance may be used for eligibility checks, result-validity decisions, or tie-breaking. Passing interface checks alone does not establish that the world model meets the quality bar.

The official evaluation uses a frozen, versioned protocol. Held-out tasks, initial states, action sequences, random seeds, reward implementation, sample counts, and aggregation parameters are not included in public integration data.

## 6. Fairness and Reproducibility

- All participant services use the same version of the official evaluator and equivalent policy-optimization budgets;
- Each official evaluation is bound to one immutable `model_version`;
- The `seed` supplied by the evaluator must be used for stochastic generation of the corresponding sample;
- Retries with the same `request_id` must be idempotent and must not be counted again as new samples;
- Weights, preprocessing, postprocessing, and dependency versions shall not be silently updated during official evaluation;
- The organizer may conduct repeated runs, review anomalous samples, and check output consistency;
- Server time, request order, source address, and opaque identifiers shall not condition prediction content.

## 7. Data and Evaluation Integrity

Participants must comply with the following requirements:

- Do not retain, distribute, or use non-public data contained in official requests for subsequent training;
- Do not use human intervention, remote human selection, or online annotation during evaluation to generate results;
- Do not probe, reverse-engineer, or exploit the official policy, reward model, held-out tasks, or scoring implementation;
- Do not hard-code outputs for public integration examples or attempt to identify official evaluation requests;
- Do not embed executable content, external-resource references, or prediction-irrelevant data in responses;
- Use only declared data, models, and dependencies for which the participant holds appropriate legal authorization.

Except for short-lived memory or caching required to complete the current request, the service should not persist request content by default. After official evaluation, request payloads in temporary caches and logs shall be deleted in accordance with the competition data policy.

## 8. Service and Submission Requirements

Participants may submit an HTTPS service accessible to the organizer or, as directed by competition notices, a container image to be hosted by the organizer. Both submission modes must expose the same API. Each submission must include at least:

- A service base URL or an immutable container-image digest;
- API credentials, submitted separately through the designated secure channel;
- The `model_version`, `api_version`, and capability manifest;
- Startup, health-check, and required-resource information;
- Licensing and compliance declarations for the model, data, and third-party components;
- A technical contact and a method for incident response during the evaluation window.

The service must remain version-stable and accessible throughout the evaluation window. Infrastructure faults may be repaired, but any change affecting model output is treated as a new submission and must be registered again.

## 9. Failed Requests and Invalid Samples

The following are recorded as failed requests or invalid samples:

- Connection failures, timeouts, or non-2xx responses;
- Invalid schema, batch, sample, shape, or data type;
- Non-finite action values in the input;
- An incorrect number of output frames or undecodable images;
- Mismatched IDs or model versions between request and response.

The evaluator may retry retryable failures a limited number of times. Persistent failures, protocol violations, version drift, or data-integrity violations may cause the evaluation run to be terminated or the submission to be invalidated.
